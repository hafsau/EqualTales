## Step-by-Step Screenshots

### 1. Landing Page
![Landing Page](demo/01_landing.png)

The landing page introduces EqualTales with key stats:
- **Age 3**: When children start absorbing stereotypes
- **Age 6**: When girls begin believing boys are smarter
- **5 Pages**: Personalized illustrated story length

Click **"Try It Now"** to begin.

---

### 2. Input Form
![Input Form](demo/02_input_form.png)

The input form allows mothers to:
- **Select a stereotype** from 10 common examples (or type custom)
- **Enter child's name** (optional, defaults to "Lily")
- **Select child's age** (3-10 years, adjusts story complexity)

Example stereotypes:
- "Girls can't do math"
- "Science is for boys"
- "Girls aren't strong enough"

---

### 3. Generation Progress
![Generation Progress](demo/03_generating.png)

Real-time progress shows 5 steps:
1. Understanding the stereotype
2. Finding a real woman who proved it wrong
3. Crafting a personalized story
4. Creating illustrations
5. Quality check

**"Featuring: [Real Woman]"** card reveals the historical figure.

---

### 4. Storybook - Page 1 (The Belief)
![Storybook Page 1](demo/04_storybook_page1.png)

The story opens with the child character expressing the stereotype they heard. The warm watercolor illustration shows the child in a relatable moment.

---

### 5. Storybook - Page 3 (The Discovery)
![Storybook Page 3](demo/05_storybook_page3.png)

Mid-story, the child discovers a real woman who defied the stereotype. Historical facts are woven into age-appropriate narrative.

---

### 6. Storybook - Page 5 (The New Belief)
![Storybook Page 5](demo/06_storybook_page5.png)

The story concludes with the child embracing a new, empowering belief. The transformation is complete.

---

### 7. Companion Section
![Companion Section](demo/07_companion.png)

After the story, parents see:
- **Discussion Prompts**: Open-ended questions to explore with their child
- **Activity Suggestion**: A hands-on activity related to the story
- **QA Score Badge**: Shows the story passed quality verification

## Sample Generated Story

**Stereotype**: "Girls can't do math"
**Child**: Lily, age 6
**Featured Woman**: Katherine Johnson (NASA mathematician)

> **Page 1 - The Belief**
> Lily loved looking at the stars. But one day at school, a boy said, "Girls can't do the math to go to space." Lily felt her heart sink.

> **Page 3 - The Discovery**
> "Let me tell you about Katherine Johnson," said Lily's mom. "She did the math that sent astronauts to the moon! NASA trusted her calculations more than their computers."

> **Page 5 - The New Belief**
> That night, Lily looked up at the moon and smiled. "If Katherine could send people to space with her math," she whispered, "maybe I can too."
